Modeling the Fracture-Flow Transition in Multiphase Granular Mixtures

This project investigates the transition between fracture-dominated and flow-dominated behavior in mixed-grain granular systems. Numerical simulations capture how grain-size distribution and pore pressure evolve to control whether a mixture fails by localized fracture or bulk flow — with implications for debris flows and landslide runout prediction.

SCOPE

Purpose
Academic research — independent study
Length
1 semester
Status
In progress
Tools
Python, numerical simulation, granular mechanics

Background

Granular materials — soils, sediments, fragmented rock — can fail in fundamentally different ways depending on their composition and loading conditions. In some cases failure is localized along discrete fracture planes; in others, the material transitions to a flowing, fluid-like state. Understanding which regime dominates has direct implications for predicting how debris flows initiate and how far they travel.

The transition between these two modes is poorly constrained, particularly in multiphase mixtures where fine and coarse grains interact with pore fluid under dynamic loading. This project develops a numerical framework to explore where and why this transition occurs.

Methods

Numerical simulations were developed to model a granular mixture subjected to progressive shear loading. Grain-size distributions and pore pressure conditions were varied systematically to explore the parameter space controlling fracture versus flow behavior. Model outputs track displacement fields, strain localization, and pore pressure evolution over time.

Simulation results were visualized as time-series animations to make the transition dynamics interpretable across parameter sets.

Results

The simulation captures the evolving fracture-flow transition as grain interactions and pore pressure feedbacks shift the dominant failure mode. At low pore pressures and coarser grain distributions, localized fracture planes emerge early and persist. As pore pressure increases or fine-grain content rises, the mixture loses coherence and transitions toward bulk flow behavior.

Discussion

These results suggest that pore pressure and grain-size distribution act as coupled controls on failure mode — small shifts in either can tip a mixture from slow, fracture-limited failure to rapid, runout-prone flow. This has practical implications for early-warning thresholds in landslide monitoring and for runout models used in hazard assessment.

Future work will expand the parameter space and test model outputs against laboratory granular experiments to validate the simulated transition behavior.
