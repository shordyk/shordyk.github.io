Machine Learning & Compound Hazards: Random Forest Prediction of Landslides in California

A random forest classifier trained to predict landslide susceptibility across California, incorporating compound hazard drivers including wildfire burn history, slope, lithology, and precipitation. The model highlights how post-fire landscapes dramatically increase shallow landslide risk, with implications for early-warning systems and land-use planning.

SCOPE

Purpose
Academic research — course project
Length
1 semester
Status
Complete
Tools
Python, scikit-learn, ArcGIS, BAER/MTBS data

Background

Landslides in California are rarely driven by a single factor. Steep terrain, unstable lithology, and intense precipitation all contribute — but wildfire adds a compounding layer of risk that traditional susceptibility models often underweight. Post-fire soils lose their cohesion and water-repellent layers form, dramatically increasing runoff and shallow landslide initiation potential during subsequent rain events.

This project investigated whether a machine learning approach incorporating fire history as a compound driver could meaningfully improve landslide susceptibility predictions over models trained on static terrain variables alone.

Methods

A random forest classifier was trained on a multi-variable dataset combining slope gradient, aspect, lithology, precipitation intensity, vegetation cover, and wildfire burn severity (derived from BAER/MTBS data). Training labels were drawn from historical landslide inventories georeferenced across the state.

Feature importance scores were extracted to assess the relative contribution of each driver. Model performance was evaluated using out-of-bag error, precision-recall curves, and spatial cross-validation to guard against geographic overfitting.

Results & Poster

The model achieved strong predictive performance, with wildfire burn severity ranking as one of the top three features by importance — confirming that post-fire conditions are a critical and underrepresented predictor of landslide susceptibility. High-risk zones were concentrated in the Coast Ranges and Southern California mountains, particularly in areas that had burned within the preceding three years.

Discussion

These findings support integrating dynamic fire-regime data into operational landslide early-warning systems. The compound nature of the hazard — fire followed by rain — means that susceptibility maps need to be updated seasonally rather than treated as static products. The random forest framework is well-suited to this because it can be retrained as new burn perimeters become available.

Limitations include spatial gaps in the historical landslide inventory and the absence of soil moisture data as a predictor. Future work could incorporate real-time satellite soil moisture products to improve temporal resolution.
