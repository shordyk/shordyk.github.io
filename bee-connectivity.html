Urban Bee Habitat Connectivity

A GIS-based analysis of pollinator habitat connectivity across an urban landscape, identifying gaps in foraging corridors and prioritizing restoration sites to support native bee populations. The project combines land cover classification, least-cost path modeling, and patch connectivity metrics to produce actionable spatial recommendations.

SCOPE

Purpose
Academic research — course project
Length
1 semester
Status
Complete
Tools
ArcGIS Pro, Python, Circuitscape

Background

Urban expansion fragments natural habitats, isolating patches of vegetation that native pollinators depend on for foraging and nesting. Bees are particularly sensitive to this fragmentation — even small gaps in the landscape can interrupt movement between habitat patches and reduce population viability over time.

This project focused on mapping and quantifying pollinator habitat connectivity within an urban study area, with the goal of identifying where targeted restoration could have the greatest impact on maintaining functional corridors for native bees.

Methods

Land cover was classified from aerial imagery to identify habitat patches — areas of flowering vegetation, parks, gardens, and urban green space — alongside barriers such as impervious surfaces and roads. A resistance surface was constructed assigning movement costs to each land cover type based on published foraging behavior data for native bee species.

Least-cost path modeling and Circuitscape current-flow analysis were used to identify likely movement corridors and pinch points where connectivity is most constrained.

Results

Connectivity analysis revealed several key pinch points where narrow vegetated corridors cross high-traffic roads and large impervious surfaces. Habitat patches in the urban core showed high isolation, while suburban areas with more continuous green space maintained stronger functional connectivity.

Discussion

Targeted restoration at identified pinch points — particularly roadside plantings and green roof installations at key crossing points — could substantially improve connectivity across the study area without requiring large land-use changes. Prioritizing native flowering species with long bloom windows would maximize foraging value for the broadest range of bee species.

This framework is transferable to other urban areas and could be extended to incorporate additional pollinator taxa or climate-projected land cover changes to make restoration planning more forward-looking.
